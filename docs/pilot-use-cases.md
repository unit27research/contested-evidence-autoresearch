# Pilot Use Cases

The best early pilots are small, source-grounded, and non-safety-critical. A pilot should test whether the field kit improves the shape of the disagreement, not whether it produces a final answer.

## Good Pilot Criteria

Use a topic when:

- at least two plausible theories compete
- at least five retrievable sources exist
- sources disagree or appear to disagree
- the decision can tolerate uncertainty
- the output can be a better question rather than a verdict
- the team can inspect primary sources

Avoid a topic when:

- the answer is already governed by a clear test
- the decision is urgent or safety-critical
- the domain requires formal review methods from the start
- the sources cannot be inspected
- the main conflict is political preference rather than evidence structure

## Recommended First Pilots

### 1. LLM Benchmark Reliability

Question:

Does a benchmark score support the broad capability claim people attach to it?

Why it fits:

- close to autoresearch and AI evals
- source-rich
- contested without needing to adjudicate a live product claim
- good demonstration of construct, contamination, scope, and metric disagreements

Possible theories:

- The benchmark measures a real general capability.
- The benchmark measures a narrower academic or task-specific skill.
- The benchmark score is partly shaped by contamination, memorization, or benchmark familiarity.
- The benchmark remains useful, but only as one signal in a multi-metric evaluation.

### 2. AI Agent Autonomy Claims

Question:

When does a demo or benchmark actually show durable agent autonomy?

Why it fits:

- strong public claims
- mixed evidence from demos, benchmarks, logs, and failures
- easy to confuse task completion with general autonomy

Possible theories:

- Agents are becoming generally autonomous across task classes.
- Reported gains are mostly benchmark- or scaffold-specific.
- Human workflow design explains much of the observed performance.
- Autonomy is real but only under narrow environmental constraints.

### 3. Policy Intervention With Mixed Evidence

Question:

Why do credible studies disagree about the effect of a specific intervention?

Why it fits:

- familiar to evidence-synthesis audiences
- often has construct, population, and timing conflicts
- shows the kit is not only for AI topics

Possible theories:

- The intervention works under specific implementation conditions.
- The apparent effect is driven by selection or measurement differences.
- The intervention affects one outcome while leaving the claimed outcome unchanged.
- The effect decays over time.

### 4. Infrastructure Or Civic System Failure

Question:

Why does a civic system continue to fail after repeated reforms?

Why it fits:

- close to the Carverton example
- can use public reports, audits, meeting records, and administrative data
- supports competing-theory mapping without requiring clinical or legal claims

Possible theories:

- Technical debt explains the failure.
- Governance churn explains the failure.
- Incentive structure explains the failure.
- User demand or operating context changed faster than the reforms.

## Pilot Output

Each pilot should produce:

- 5-10 evidence cards
- 2-4 theory pages
- 1-2 study autopsies
- 3-5 research questions
- 1 short cycle report

The cycle report should answer:

- What got clearer?
- What conflict remains?
- Which broad claim should be narrowed?
- What evidence should be collected next?
- What should not be concluded yet?
